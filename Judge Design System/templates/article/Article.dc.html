The Slow Craft of Judging Fast

Evaluation used to be the checkbox at the end. We rebuilt it as the thing that shapes the work — legible, first-class, and quick enough to run on every change.

Mara Reyes Byline ▾

For most of the last decade, the teams we admired treated evaluation as hygiene — something you did once the interesting work was finished, a gate rather than a lens. It kept the lights on and told you almost nothing about how the thing was actually made.

The premise of NAV / Judge is stubbornly simple: judgment should come first, and it should be legible. Not a score buried in a dashboard, but a page you can read — one that moves at the speed of the work it describes. When the evaluation is the artifact, everything downstream gets sharper.

Reading Like a Page

We kept coming back to print. A well-set article does something software rarely bothers with: it establishes a hierarchy so clear you can skim it or sink into it, and either way you know exactly where you are. Type is the interface. The headline sets the stakes, the subhead makes the promise, the body keeps it.

Nothing hidden, nothing decorative — just the exciting stuff, delivered since the turn of the century.

— NAV / Judge, founding note

That constraint turned out to be liberating. Once the evaluation had to read, we stopped adding chrome and started removing it. The condensed, all-caps display face does the shouting; the serif body does the thinking. Between them there's very little left to decorate, which is exactly the point.

Fast Enough to Matter

Speed is what makes judgment first-class rather than ceremonial. If a review takes a day, it happens once. If it takes seconds, it happens on every change — and a system you consult constantly is one you actually trust. That's the whole wager, and so far it's paying out. ■
